Electrons were discovered in 1896 by the English physicist J. J. Thomson

He also discovered that electrons were much smaller and lighter than atoms.

An early theory called the plum-pudding model regarded the atom as a positively charged sphere in which the negative electrons were distributed.

The experiments by Geiger and Marsden on the scattering of $\alpha$-particles from thin metal films provided evidence for the nuclear model of the atom.

The behaviour in this experiment was then explained by Ernest Rutherford

* Shortly after Rutherford, **Niels Bohr** a Danish physicist, developed a better idea to explain how an atom emits light. * He suggested that the electrons circled the nucleus at high speed being kept in certain defined orbits * He pictured atoms as miniature solar systems electrons may jump to an outer orbit. * The atom is then said to be **excited**. * Very soon afterwards the electrons return to an inner orbit * energy is then thrown out by bursts of photons as infrared light, ultraviolet or X-rays.

The wavelength of the radiation emitted depends on the two orbits between which the electrons jump.

If an atom gains enough energy for an electron to escape altogether, the atom becomes an ion and the energy needed to achieve this is called the ionisation energy of the atom.

* Although it remains useful for some purposes, the Rutherford–Bohr model was replaced by a mathematical model developed by Erwin Schrödinger * The best we can do, without using advanced mathematics, is to say that in the Schrödinger model the atom consists of a nucleus surrounded by a hazy cloud of electrons. * This theory does away with the idea of electrons moving in definite orbits and replaces them by energy levels that are different for each element.

Regions of the atom where the mathematics predicts that electrons are more likely to be found are represented by denser shading

* German physicist Otto Hahn split a uranium nucleus and found barium in the debris of his experiment. * Nuclear fission releases a huge amount of energy and the Hungarian scientist * Leo Szilard, also working in exile, realised that this could be used to make a bomb. * The fear was that Nazi scientists would create this bomb, allowing them to win the war. * Szilard enlisted the help of Einstein in writing to the US president to persuade him of the necessity of developing the bomb before the Nazis did.
* After the bombing of Pearl Harbor the USA joined the war. Within a year the Manhattan Project successfully produced the chain reaction needed for the bomb. In 1945, bombs were dropped causing thousands of deaths in Hiroshima and Nagasaki in Japan. * J. Robert Oppenheimer described the bomb using words from the Bhagavad Gita **'Now I am become Death, the destroyer of worlds’.** * There are now about 27 000 nuclear bombs in the world.

An atom consists of a positively charged nucleus and negatively charged electrons in orbit around the nucleus

J.J. Thomson made a model of the atom known as the plum pudding model

In this model, atoms are a positively charged sphere in which electrons are embedded

Rutherford's alpha particle scattering experiment

The experiment shows some particles pass through without deflection, some deflect a little, some deflect a lot

These observations are explained by Rutherford's model as shown above

The scattering of alpha ($\alpha$) particles by a sheet of thin metal supports the nuclear model of the atom, by providing evidence for:

  • a very small nucleus surrounded by mostly empty space
  • a nucleus containing most of the mass of the atom
  • a nucleus that is positively charged

Atoms may form positive ions by losing electrons or form negative ions by gaining electrons

Representing an element like $^{A}_{Z}\text{X}$ is known as nuclide notation

The proton number $A$ is the relative charge on the nucleus.

The relative mass of a nucleus is roughly equal to its nucleon number $Z$.

An isotope is a version of a chemical element with the same number of protons but a different number of neutrons than other versions of that element.

Some nuclei are not stable, so they decay by emitting radiation to more stable elements

The emission of radiation from a nucleus is spontaneous and random in direction

Background radiation is the average amount of radiation that people are exposed to in a year, and it comes from both natural and man-made sources

Some types of radiation ionize the matter they pass through. This is known as ionizing radiation

Ionizing Radiation takes the form of alpha, beta, and gamma radiation

  • Alpha Radiation: consists of a helium ion (2 protons and 2 neutrons)
  • Beta Radiation: consists of electrons
  • Gamma Radiation: consists of electromagnetic waves of similar wavelength as X-rays

Alpha radiation: blocked very easily but has the most ionizing power

Gamma radiation: difficult to block but ionizes only weakly

Beta radiation: medium penetration and blocked by most metals

Ionizing Radiation can be detected with a Geiger-MÃ¼ller tube

A GM counter is a gas-filled tube with electrodes that have a high voltage between them. When radiation passes through the tube, it collides with the gas, knocking an electron away from the atom. This process is called ionization. The electron is attracted to the central electrode, which is positively charged, and a small current pulse is produced. An electronic circuit counts the pulses and displays them on a screen.

Note: You will have to be familiar with problems calculating count rate and involving background radiation

A cloud chamber can be used to view the path of radiation

Cloud chambers can detect ionising particles, and alpha-particles leave a thick and recognisable track due to their relatively high ionisation power.
  • Alpha particles are positive and deflect slightly towards a negative plate
  • Beta particle are negative and deflect a lot towards a positive plate
  • Gamma rays are not charged and do not deflect at all

The deflection of radiation in a magnetic field can be found using Fleming's left hand rule

  • Alpha rays: the direction of current is in the same direction of the radiation
  • Beta rays: the direction of current is opposite the direction of the radiation
  • Gamma rays: do not deflect

Alpha decay: a helium ion is ejected from the nucleus

$$^{A}_{Z}\text{X} \to ^{A-4}_{Z-2}\text{Y} + ^{4}_{2}\alpha$$

Beta decay: a neutron decays into a proton and an electron, and the electron is ejected

$$^{A}_{Z}\text{X} \to ^{A}_{Z+1}\text{Y} + ^{0}_{-1}\text{e}$$

Gamma decay: an excited atom gets rid of excess energy in the form of gamma rays

$$^{A}_{Z}\text{X*} \to ^{A}_{Z}\text{X} + \gamma$$

The half-life of an isotope is the time taken for half the nuclei of that isotope in any sample to decay

  • After one half-life: one-half of the isotope will be left
  • After two half-lives: one-fourth of the isotope will be left
  • After three half-lives: one-eighth of the isotope will be left
  • After four half-lives: one-sixteenth of the isotope will be left

Smoke detectors that use alpha radiation work by ionizing the air with alpha particles, which creates a small electric current

Alpha particles emitted from a radioactive material, like americium-241, ionize the air between two charged metal plates. The ions are attracted to the oppositely charged plates, causing a current to flow. When smoke enters the detector, it absorbs some of the alpha particles, which reduces the current flow. This triggers the alarm.

Beta radiation is the most common type of radiation used to measure the thickness of materials because it has the right amount of penetrating power

To measure thickness, a radioactive source emits beta radiation onto one side of a material, and a detector is placed on the other side. If the material's thickness doesn't change, the activity level remains constant. If the thickness changes, the activity level increases or decreases, which can trigger rollers to adjust the material's thickness

In nuclear fission, atoms are split apart, which releases energy according to the equation $E= mc^2$

Uranium-235 is a common fissile elements which commonly breaks into barium-141 and krypton-92

$$^{235}_{92}\text{U} + ^{1}_{0}\text{n} \to ^{141}_{56}\text{Ba} + ^{92}_{36}\text{Kr} + 3 ^{1}_{0}\text{n}$$

A nuclear fission chain reaction is a self-sustaining process that occurs when neutrons released from a fission reaction split other fissionable nuclei, releasing more neutrons:

  • A neutron collides with a fissionable nucleus, such as uranium, and splits it
  • The split nucleus releases energy in the form of heat and radiation, and additional neutrons
  • The released neutrons collide with other fissionable nuclei, splitting them and releasing more neutrons, repeating the process

The chain reaction is self-sustaining when the number of neutrons released equals or exceeds the number of neutrons lost. This happens when the mass of fissile isotopes exceeds a certain critical mass

Barium control rods are used to absorb neutrons which will reduce the rate of fission

The heat released from a nuclear reactor is used to power a steam engine which will generate energy

The nuclei of Iron and Nickel are the most stable. All other elements will eventually try to become these.

  • Very heavy elements decay or fission into stable elements
  • Very light elements fuse to become heavier to reach these stable elements
